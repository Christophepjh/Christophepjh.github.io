Introduction

The Future of Meat

In 2013 the first lab grown meat burger was eaten on live TV. Over $300,000 was spent with reseach spanning 2 years, the result being a single burger which food critics described as indistinguishable to real meat. Despite the slow and expensive start, the lab grown meat sector is booming. Today, over 60 firms are developing some form of lab grown meat.

In this project, I will explore the lab grown meat market through three sections:

1. Motivation behind the development of lab grown meat

2. Innovation and evolution of the meat industry

3. Adaptation that will be required to move towards lab grown meat

Discussions about my Data can be found at the bottom of the page.

Motivation

What problems can lab grown meat solve?

In this section I will look at the trends in global meat production and consumption.

This scatter bubble chart shows the relationship between a nations wealth and meat consumption. A clear positive trend is observed between wealth and meat consumption. Many of the worlds most populous nations, notably India, have low meat consumption. As these nations develop, demand for meat will rise.

These maps show the rise in the worlds most popular protein producing animals. The prominence of meat production in China is evident. 83% of the globes agricultural land use is dedicated to meat and dairy production, taking up to 30% of the earths land surface and producing 18% of our greenhouse gas emitions.

Food security is a real issue for countries that have little land. This API shows the difference between UK meat imports and exports. On average, the UK imports three times more meat than it exports. Lab grown meat production would be far less dependent on land area.

Innovation

Is lab grown meat a feasible alternative?

In this section I look at the past innovations in the meat market and developments in the lab grown meat sector.

We have turned the broiler chicken into a meat producing machine. Data scraped from the US National Chicken Council shows the evolution of the broiler chicken from 1925 to 2021. Chickens today produce more meat, die less, develop faster, and eat less food than their ancestors. However, this development is slowing. Approximately 69 billion chickens are killed every year, a quickly rising statistic.

Lab grown meat producers are developing a wide range of meat products. Data scraped from Wikipedia shows where and what lab grown meat producers are making. Silicon Valley, Israel and the Netherlands are pioneering development. Singapore is leading in the adoption of lab grown meat.

Many market analysts predict lab grown meat will never be widely adopted due to its high production costs. This bar chart shows the predicted costs of producing lab grown meat in the US. Cell culture mediums, bioreactors and labour make up the largest costs. With current technology, the minimum cost of lab grown meat is 63$/KG.

Adaptation

How will we transition to lab grown meat?

Lab grown meats will never be widely adopted if consumers are suspicious of them. A common barrier is known as the 'yuk' factor as consumers are sceptical of the meats hygeine.

This graph from a survey of 1,930 UK adults shows attitudes towards lab grown meats by age, as well as other alternative proteins. Their is consumer hesitancy towards lab grown meat with 43% unwilling to try it. However, younger consumers are more open to them.

A shift to lab grown meat will require a different workforce. This donut chart shows the professions required for a lab grown meat firm to produce 540,000KG of meat - equivalent to 231,000 US chickens.

Conclusion

These graphs have shown the past, present and future of the meat industry. For lab grown meats to become viable alternatives to traditional meat, producers need to fix the cost barrier, consumers will need to overcome the 'yuk' factor, and farmers will need to become scientists. This transition has to happen to avoid the increasing environmental costs of meat production.
